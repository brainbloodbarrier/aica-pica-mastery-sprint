## Conventional Arteriography

### Why Conventional Angiography Matters

- **Gold standard** for visualizing posterior circulation
- Best temporal resolution for dynamic flow patterns
- Essential for endovascular treatment planning
- Required for complex aneurysm and AVM cases

---

### Key Resource: Neuroangio.org

**Website**: [https://neuroangio.org](https://neuroangio.org)

**Description**: Comprehensive online atlas of neuroangiography with detailed anatomical descriptions and high-quality images.

#### AICA-Specific Pages:

1. **AICA Anatomy & Variants**
   - URL: `https://neuroangio.org/anatomy-and-variants/aica/`
   - **What to look for**:
     - Origin from basilar artery (usually lower/middle third)
     - Single vs duplicate vs triplicate origins
     - Course through CPA toward internal auditory canal
     - Meatal loop configuration (reaches or enters IAC in 50%)
     - Bifurcation into rostral and caudal trunks
     - Anastomoses with SCA (rostral) and PICA (caudal)

2. **AICA Labyrinthine Artery**
   - URL: `https://neuroangio.org/anatomy-and-variants/aica/labyrinthine-artery/`
   - **What to look for**:
     - Origin from AICA (usually premeatal segment, 77%)
     - Course into internal auditory canal with CN VII/VIII
     - Number of labyrinthine arteries (most have 2, range 1-4)
     - Relationship to meatal loop

3. **AICA Loops**
   - URLs:
     - `https://neuroangio.org/anatomy-and-variants/aica/aica-loop-01/`
     - `https://neuroangio.org/anatomy-and-variants/aica/aica-loop-02/`
   - **What to look for**:
     - Meatal loop configuration (medial loop)
     - Loop extent relative to porus acousticus
     - Subarcuate loop (second "M" configuration toward subarcuate fossa)
     - Vertical vs horizontal loop orientation

4. **AICA Duplication**
   - URL: `https://neuroangio.org/anatomy-and-variants/aica/aica-duplicated/`
   - **What to look for**:
     - Two separate AICA origins from basilar artery (26%)
     - Rostral vs caudal duplicate identification
     - Which duplicate gives nerve-related branches (usually rostral)
     - Territory distribution

### Arteriographic Views & Projections

#### Standard Projections:

1. **AP (Anteroposterior) View**
   - **AICA**: Visualizes origin from basilar, lateral course to CPA
   - **PICA**: Shows medial-to-lateral course around medulla and tonsil
   - **Best for**: Identifying origins, assessing bilateral symmetry

2. **Lateral View**
   - **AICA**: Shows anterior-to-posterior course through CPA, meatal loops
   - **PICA**: Demonstrates caudal and cranial loops, relationship to foramen magnum
   - **Best for**: Loop configurations, bifurcation patterns, vertical positions

3. **Oblique Views**
   - **Working projections**: Tailored to specific anatomy for aneurysm/AVM evaluation
   - **Best for**: Separating overlapping vessels, visualizing aneurysm necks

#### Key Phases:

- **Arterial Phase**: AICA and PICA fill and opacify
- **Capillary Phase**: Cerebellar blush, perforator territories
- **Venous Phase**: Venous drainage patterns (not focus of arterial anatomy study)

## MRI-Angiography (MRA)

### Why MRA Matters

- **Non-invasive** vascular imaging
- Excellent for **preoperative surgical planning**
- Can be combined with high-resolution anatomical MRI
- Time-of-flight (TOF) and contrast-enhanced techniques

---

### Key MRA Techniques

#### 1. Time-of-Flight (TOF) MRA
- **Principle**: Flowing blood appears bright without contrast
- **Best for**: Visualizing medium-to-large vessels
- **Limitations**: Slow flow may be underestimated

#### 2. Contrast-Enhanced MRA
- **Principle**: Gadolinium contrast enhances vascular signal
- **Best for**: Higher spatial resolution, better small vessel visualization
- **Timing**: Arterial phase acquisition critical

#### 3. High-Resolution 3D TOF
- **Best for**: Posterior circulation, small perforators
- **Protocols**: Thin slices (0.5-1mm), high matrix

---

### What to Look For on MRA

#### AICA Assessment:

1. **Origin & Course**
   - Identify origin point on basilar artery (distance from VA junction)
   - Count number of AICA trunks (single, duplicate, triplicate)
   - Trace course toward internal auditory canal

2. **Meatal Loop**
   - Does loop reach or enter the IAC? (critical for CPA surgery)
   - Loop configuration relative to porus acousticus
   - Relationship to CN VII/VIII (best seen on high-res anatomical MRI)

3. **Bifurcation**
   - Identify rostral and caudal trunks
   - Bifurcation timing (before or after nerves)

4. **Labyrinthine Artery**
   - Often not visualized on standard MRA (too small)
   - May be seen on high-resolution 3D TOF
   - Infer from AICA premeatal/meatal segment anatomy

#### PICA Assessment:

1. **Origin & Segments**
   - Measure distance from foramen magnum (critical for far-lateral approaches)
   - Assess caliber (hypoplastic vs normal vs dominant)
   - Trace five segments if possible

2. **Caudal Loop**
   - Position relative to foramen magnum (above, at, or below)
   - Measure vertical extent
   - **Surgical planning**: If loop is below FM, will encounter during C1 arch removal

3. **Cranial Loop**
   - Position between tonsil and fourth ventricle
   - Apex height relative to fastigium
   - **Surgical planning**: Defines lateral boundary for telovelar approach

4. **Bifurcation**
   - Identify medial and lateral trunks
   - Bifurcation site (relative to tonsil)
   - Early vs late bifurcation (affects telovelar approach complexity)

5. **Absence vs Presence**
   - 16% absent - critical to identify preoperatively
   - If absent, assess AICA for territory compensation
   - Check contralateral PICA for bihemispheric supply

## CT-Angiography (CTA)

### Why CTA Matters

- **Fast acquisition** (critical in acute stroke/trauma)
- **High spatial resolution** with modern multi-detector CT
- **Bone detail** (useful for surgical planning with bony landmarks)
- **3D reconstruction** for surgical navigation

---

### CTA Technique Considerations

#### Optimal CTA Protocol:
- **Slice thickness**: â‰¤0.625mm (isotropic voxels)
- **Contrast timing**: Bolus tracking or test bolus for arterial phase
- **Coverage**: Skull base to vertex (include foramen magnum for PICA)
- **Reconstructions**: Axial, coronal, sagittal, MIP, 3D volume rendering

#### Windowing:
- **Soft tissue window**: Relationship to brainstem/cerebellum
- **Bone window**: Relationship to petrous bone, IAC, foramen magnum
- **Vascular window**: Optimize vessel contrast

---

### What to Look For on CTA

#### AICA Assessment (same as MRA, plus):

1. **Bone Landmarks**
   - Meatal loop relationship to IAC (use bone window)
   - Subarcuate fossa identification
   - Petrous apex relationships

2. **3D Surgical Planning**
   - Rotate 3D volume rendering to simulate surgical view
   - Retrosigmoid approach angle: visualize AICA-nerve relationships
   - Translabyrinthine approach: AICA loop inside canal risk

#### PICA Assessment (same as MRA, plus):

1. **Foramen Magnum Relationships**
   - Caudal loop position relative to FM and C1 arch (use bone window)
   - Critical for far-lateral approach planning
   - Identify extradural origin if present

2. **Fourth Ventricle Relationships**
   - Cranial loop position relative to fourth ventricle boundaries
   - Tonsil position and PICA course
   - Telovelar corridor assessment

3. **Surgical Approach Simulation**
   - 3D reconstructions from approach angle
   - Visualize structures as they'll appear in surgery
   - Bone removal planning (e.g., C1 arch, mastoid)

## Endovascular Anatomy

### Why Endovascular Perspective Matters

- Understanding **catheter navigation** to AICA/PICA
- **Aneurysm treatment** (coiling, flow diversion)
- **Diagnostic angiography** interpretation
- **Stroke intervention** (mechanical thrombectomy)

---

### Catheter Navigation

#### Accessing AICA:
1. Femoral artery access
2. Aortic arch navigation
3. Selective VA or BA catheterization
4. **Challenges**: Tortuous basilar artery, acute AICA takeoff angles

#### Accessing PICA:
1. Femoral artery access
2. Aortic arch navigation
3. Selective VA catheterization (ipsilateral to PICA of interest)
4. **Challenges**: Extradural origin, acute angle takeoff, small caliber

---

### Neuroangio.org Endovascular Resources

#### AICA Endovascular:
- URL: `https://neuroangio.org/interventional-stroke/posterior-circulation/`
- **What to look for**:
  - Catheter tip positions for AICA injections
  - Microcatheter navigation into AICA for aneurysm treatment
  - Collateral flow patterns if AICA occluded

#### PICA Endovascular:
- URL: `https://neuroangio.org/interventional-stroke/vertebral-pica/`
- **What to look for**:
  - VA-PICA junction anatomy
  - PICA origin angles
  - Perforator visualization from PICA
  - Vertebral dissection extending to PICA origin

---

### Aneurysm-Specific Imaging

#### VA-PICA Aneurysms:
- **Location**: Junction of VA and PICA origin (common)
- **Imaging goals**:
  - Aneurysm size, neck, dome orientation
  - PICA origin relationship (incorporated vs separate)
  - Perforators from aneurysm wall
  - Working angle for clipping or coiling

#### AICA Aneurysms:
- **Location**: Basilar-AICA junction, premeatal segment (rare)
- **Imaging goals**:
  - Relationship to CN VII/VIII
  - Labyrinthine artery origin
  - Perforator origins
  - Surgical vs endovascular approach decision

## 3D Reconstruction & Surgical Planning

### Software & Tools

#### 1. OsiriX / Horos (Mac)
- Free DICOM viewer with 3D reconstruction
- **Use for**: Rotating CTA/MRA datasets, multiplanar reformats

#### 2. 3D Slicer
- Open-source medical image analysis
- **Use for**: Advanced segmentation, surgical planning

#### 3. Commercial Planning Software
- BrainLab, Medtronic StealthStation
- **Use for**: Intraoperative navigation planning

---

### 3D Reconstruction Workflow

1. **Load CTA or MRA dataset** (thin-slice acquisition)
2. **Segment vessels** (automatic or manual segmentation)
3. **Isolate AICA and PICA** (remove other vessels for clarity)
4. **Create 3D volume rendering**
5. **Rotate to surgical approach angle**
6. **Measure key distances** (e.g., PICA caudal loop to FM, AICA loop to porus)
7. **Export for surgical planning conference**

---

### Surgical Approach-Specific Views

#### Retrosigmoid CPA Approach:
- **View from**: Posterior-lateral, 45Â° from midline
- **Structures to identify**: AICA premeatal segment, meatal loop, CN VII/VIII
- **Rotate dataset** to match surgical angle

#### Telovelar Approach:
- **View from**: Inferior, looking superiorly into fourth ventricle
- **Structures to identify**: Bilateral PICA cranial loops, tonsils, tela choroidea
- **Assess**: Working corridor between PICAs

#### Far-Lateral Approach:
- **View from**: Posterolateral, below and lateral to foramen magnum
- **Structures to identify**: PICA caudal loop, origin from VA, lower cranial nerves
- **Measure**: Caudal loop position relative to FM and C1 arch

## Case-Based Learning

### Suggested Case Study Approach

For each imaging case you review:

1. **Identify the imaging modality** (angiogram, MRA, CTA)
2. **Locate AICA**:
   - Origin from basilar artery
   - Course through CPA
   - Meatal loop configuration
   - Bifurcation pattern
   - Any variations (duplicate, loops)

3. **Locate PICA**:
   - Origin from vertebral artery (measure from FM)
   - Five segments (if visible)
   - Caudal loop (position relative to FM)
   - Cranial loop (position in fourth ventricle)
   - Bifurcation pattern
   - Presence vs absence

4. **Assess surgical implications**:
   - For CPA tumor: AICA meatal loop risk?
   - For fourth ventricle lesion: PICA cranial loop position?
   - For foramen magnum lesion: PICA caudal loop below FM?
   - For aneurysm: Perforators? Vessel incorporated?

## Quick Reference Tables

### Summary of Imaging Modalities

| Modality | Strengths | Limitations | Best Use Case |
|----------|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Conventional Angiography** | Gold standard, dynamic flow, best resolution | Invasive, radiation, contrast | Aneurysms, AVMs, endovascular planning |
| **MRA (TOF)** | Non-invasive, no radiation, no contrast | Slow flow underestimation, artifacts | Preoperative screening, surgical planning |
| **MRA (Contrast)** | Better small vessel visualization | Requires contrast, timing critical | Small vessel assessment, perforators |
| **CTA** | Fast, excellent resolution, bone detail | Radiation, contrast, artifacts from bone | Acute stroke, surgical planning with bone landmarks |
| **3D CTA/MRA** | Surgical simulation, angle rotation | Requires post-processing | Approach planning, intraoperative navigation |

---

### Key Measurements on Imaging

| Measurement | Technique | Clinical Significance |
|-------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **PICA origin from FM** | Sagittal MRA/CTA, measure on midline VA | Far-lateral approach planning: if >15mm below or low origin, may affect exposure |
| **PICA caudal loop from FM** | Sagittal view, measure loop apex to FM line | If <0 (below FM), will encounter during C1 arch removal |
| **AICA meatal loop to porus** | Axial CTA with bone window | If loop enters IAC, at risk during IAC drilling for acoustic neuroma |
| **PICA diameter at origin** | Axial source images, measure at origin | <1mm suggests hypoplasia, >3mm suggests dominant PICA |
| **AICA-PICA territory balance** | Compare AICA and PICA sizes bilaterally | Asymmetric suggests compensation if one absent/hypoplastic |

### Imaging Checklist for Surgical Planning

#### For CPA Tumor (Retrosigmoid Approach):

- [ ] AICA origin identified
- [ ] AICA course to IAC traced
- [ ] Meatal loop configuration assessed (enters IAC: yes/no)
- [ ] AICA bifurcation identified
- [ ] Duplicate AICA ruled out
- [ ] PICA caudal trunk position noted (if relevant)
- [ ] Venous anatomy reviewed (superior petrosal vein, petrosal vein complex)

#### For Fourth Ventricle Tumor (Telovelar Approach):

- [ ] Bilateral PICA cranial loops identified
- [ ] Cranial loop positions symmetric or asymmetric
- [ ] PICA bifurcation locations (early vs late)
- [ ] Working corridor between PICAs assessed
- [ ] PICA absence ruled out (would affect approach)
- [ ] Tonsil positions and size assessed

#### For Foramen Magnum Lesion (Far-Lateral Approach):

- [ ] PICA origin measured from FM
- [ ] PICA caudal loop position relative to FM (above/at/below)
- [ ] Extradural PICA origin ruled out
- [ ] VA course and dominance assessed
- [ ] Lower cranial nerve positions (MRI, not angio)
- [ ] C1 arch and occipital condyle anatomy (CTA bone window)

#### For VA-PICA Aneurysm:

- [ ] Aneurysm size and neck measured
- [ ] PICA origin relationship (incorporated vs separate)
- [ ] Perforators from PICA or aneurysm visualized
- [ ] Working angle for clipping determined
- [ ] Endovascular access feasibility assessed
- [ ] Contralateral VA and PICA assessed (for tolerance of sacrifice)

## Self-Assessment Questions

After reviewing imaging resources, test your knowledge:

1. **On a lateral vertebral artery angiogram, how do you differentiate PICA's caudal loop from its cranial loop?**
   - Answer: Caudal loop is inferior, wrapping around lower tonsil pole. Cranial loop is superior, between tonsil and fourth ventricle. The cranial loop apex points toward the fastigium.

2. **A CTA shows AICA's meatal loop extending into the medial half of the IAC. What is the surgical implication?**
   - Answer: High risk during IAC drilling for acoustic neuroma. The loop must be identified and gently mobilized before bone removal to prevent hearing loss from labyrinthine artery injury.

3. **On MRA, PICA is absent on the right. What should you assess on the left?**
   - Answer: Check if the left PICA is dominant and crosses midline to supply the right (azygous PICA). Also assess if right AICA is enlarged to compensate for absent PICA territory.

4. **How can you identify extradural PICA origin on sagittal CTA?**
   - Answer: Look for PICA arising from VA below the foramen magnum level, coursing superiorly behind the atlantooccipital joint, then penetrating dura at the level of the lateral medulla. Use bone window to correlate with bony landmarks.

5. **What is the minimum CTA slice thickness needed for reliable 3D reconstruction of AICA and PICA?**
   - Answer: â‰¤0.625mm (ideally 0.5mm or less) with isotropic voxels. Thicker slices lose small vessel detail and loops.

## Integration with Anatomical Knowledge

### Correlating Imaging with Surgical Anatomy

**Before surgery**:
1. Review preoperative MRA/CTA
2. Identify AICA and PICA using anatomical knowledge from study guides
3. Measure key parameters (loop positions, origin points, bifurcations)
4. Create mental 3D model of expected surgical anatomy
5. Anticipate variations and plan accordingly

**During surgery**:
1. Compare actual anatomy with preoperative imaging
2. Note any discrepancies (imaging limitations, patient positioning effects)
3. Use microsurgical Doppler to confirm vessel identity if uncertain

**After surgery** (if complications):
1. Review postoperative imaging
2. Correlate with intraoperative events
3. Learn from any anatomical surprises
